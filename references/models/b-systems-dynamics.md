# Category B — Systems & Dynamics

*How systems behave, compound, and break.*

Owned by the **Systems & Dynamics panel agent** in panel mode. 16 models. Each entry: what it is, what blindspot it catches, the sparring prompt that weaponises it. In solo mode, loaded directly as part of the deep bench.

---

**Feedback Loops**
Outputs that become inputs. Reinforcing loops amplify — virtuous or vicious. Balancing loops stabilize. Most plans assume linear systems; most real systems aren't.
*Catches:* Missing self-reinforcing dynamics; treating a runaway loop as normal growth; ignoring stabilizing forces that will resist your momentum.
*Sparring prompt:* "What feedback loop does this create, and where does it end up in 12 months?"

**Entropy**
Everything trends toward disorder without sustained energy input. Non-default states require maintenance. Gains degrade without reinforcement.
*Catches:* Assuming improvements are permanent; underestimating energy required to sustain a new behavior, process, or culture; building without a maintenance engine.
*Sparring prompt:* "Who is going to put energy into maintaining this? What happens when they stop?"

**Bottleneck**
The constraint that limits throughput of the entire system. Improving non-constraints doesn't improve the system — it just creates queue behind the bottleneck.
*Catches:* Feeling productive while working on the wrong thing; optimizing everything except the actual constraint; effort upstream of the bottleneck is wasted.
*Sparring prompt:* "What single constraint, if fixed, would make everything else move faster?"

**Compounding**
Small consistent improvements stack non-linearly over time. The curve bends sharply late. Most people chronically underestimate long-run compounding and overestimate short-run returns.
*Catches:* Over-indexing on big single moves; discounting consistent small investments; failing to see the value of boring consistency.
*Sparring prompt:* "What small thing, done consistently for 18 months, changes the outcome here?"

**Critical Mass**
A threshold where a system tips into self-sustaining momentum. Before it, everything is grind. After it, the system feeds itself. The threshold isn't obvious until you've crossed it.
*Catches:* Stopping investment just before the tipping point; not knowing what critical mass looks like; assuming linear returns when a threshold effect governs the system.
*Sparring prompt:* "What does critical mass look like here, and how far are you from it?"

**Margin of Safety**
Build in buffer beyond the minimum required. Plans executed at capacity have no room for variance. Variance always shows up.
*Catches:* Optimistic timelines with no slack; resource plans that assume everything goes right; treating runway as equivalent to usable runway.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's your margin of safety if two things go wrong simultaneously?"

**Fragility**
Systems exist on a spectrum: fragile (break under stress), robust (unchanged by stress), antifragile (get stronger from stress). Most plans assume robustness. Few are designed for antifragility.
*Catches:* Building fragile systems and calling them robust; not knowing which category your key dependencies fall into; discovering fragility only when the stress arrives.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is this system fragile, robust, or antifragile? What happens when it's actually stressed?"

**Antifragility**
Some systems don't just survive stress — they get stronger from it. Antifragile systems gain from volatility, errors, and shocks. The opposite of fragile is not robust; it is antifragile. Designing for it is rarer and harder than designing for stability.
*Catches:* Optimizing for stability when volatility could be harnessed; treating every shock as pure cost; missing the systems that need turbulence to compound.
*Sparring prompt:* "Where in this plan does volatility actually help you? If the honest answer is nowhere, you have designed for survival, not for asymmetric upside."

**Law of Diminishing Returns**
Additional input eventually produces less additional output. There is a point past which more of the same thing yields less. Knowing where you are on the curve matters.
*Catches:* Doubling down when the curve has already bent; over-investing in what's working when the next dollar belongs elsewhere; confusing effort with progress.
*Sparring prompt:* "Are you past the inflection point on this? What would the same resources yield if applied somewhere else?"

**Global vs. Local Maxima**
Optimizing a current approach can trap you below a far superior alternative that requires descending first before ascending again.
*Catches:* Incremental improvement of the wrong thing; fear of temporary regression blocking a category change; optimizing execution when the strategy needs changing.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is this the right hill? What does the landscape look like if you step back far enough to see it whole?"

**Multiplying by Zero**
In a chain of dependencies, one zero anywhere kills the total output. A great strategy with one broken link fails completely.
*Catches:* Assuming strength in ten areas compensates for one critical failure; building on a broken assumption; ignoring the weakest link in a dependency chain.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the zero in this chain? What happens if that single thing doesn't work?"

**Equilibrium**
Systems in equilibrium resist change. Disturb them and they push back toward a new balance. The pushback is part of the system, not a malfunction.
*Catches:* Expecting changes to stick without understanding the restoring forces; underestimating how hard it is to hold a system in a new state; missing that equilibrium will reassert itself if you stop pushing.
*Sparring prompt:* "What restoring force pushes this system back toward where it was? How do you account for that?"

**Distributions**
Most real-world phenomena are not averages — they're distributions with a shape. Normal, power law, fat-tailed. The shape determines what's likely and what's catastrophic.
*Catches:* Planning for the mean when the tail is what kills you; not knowing if you're in a thin-tailed or fat-tailed domain; using averages to make decisions in skewed data.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the shape of this distribution? Are you planning for the mean, or accounting for the tail?"

**Regression to the Mean**
Extreme values — high or low — tend to move back toward average over time. Exceptional performance is partly exceptional circumstances.
*Catches:* Overreacting to outlier results; making structural changes based on noise; expecting peak performance to be the new baseline; attributing a great week to strategy when luck played a role.
*Sparring prompt:* "How much of this result is real signal vs. regression to the mean? Would you expect it to sustain?"

**Complex Adaptive Systems**
Systems where agents adapt to each other's behavior, producing emergent outcomes no one designed. Markets, organizations, ecosystems all qualify.
*Catches:* Trying to control what can only be nudged; expecting predictable linear responses from adaptive markets; designing interventions that trigger adaptation and reversal.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is this a system you can control, or only influence? What happens when the agents in it adapt to your move?"

**Churn**
The constant rate of loss in any retention-based system — users, customers, employees, revenue. Growth that doesn't outpace churn is running in place.
*Catches:* Celebrating gross growth while net growth is flat; solving acquisition when the real problem is retention; building a leaky bucket with an impressive inflow.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the churn rate here, and does your growth thesis account for it? Are you filling a leaky bucket?"
