# Category D — Strategy & Markets

*How competition, value, and disruption work.*

Owned by the **Strategy & Markets panel agent** in panel mode. 14 models. Each entry: what it is, what blindspot it catches, the sparring prompt that weaponises it. In solo mode, loaded directly as part of the deep bench.

---

**One Thing (80/20 / Pareto)**
20% of inputs produce 80% of outputs. In every system, a small number of things drive most of the results.
*Catches:* Treating all goals equally; spreading resources across everything instead of concentrating on the few things that move the needle.
*Sparring prompt:* "If you could only do one thing to move this forward, what would it be? Are you doing it?"

**Incentives**
People and systems reliably do what they're rewarded for — even when that wasn't the design intent. Show me the incentive and I'll show you the behavior.
*Catches:* Designing systems that produce the wrong behavior; expecting behavior change without incentive change; being surprised when people optimize for the metric, not the goal.
*Sparring prompt:* "What are the actual incentives for everyone in this system? What will they do when you're not watching?"

**Red Queen Effect**
In competitive systems, you must keep improving just to stay in place. Standing still is falling behind. The baseline moves whether you move with it or not.
*Catches:* Mistaking "we're improving" for "we're winning" when competitors improve faster; treating capability-building as optional; assuming a lead is stable.
*Sparring prompt:* "How fast is the competitive bar rising? Is your rate of improvement faster or slower than the bar?"

**Trade-offs**
Every choice has an opportunity cost. You can't have everything — and pretending you can is itself a choice with real consequences.
*Catches:* Treating decisions as if they have no cost; failing to name what you're giving up; building strategies that quietly require two incompatible things simultaneously.
*Sparring prompt:* "What are you not doing by doing this? Name the trade-off explicitly."

**Opportunity Cost**
Every choice forecloses the best alternative. The real cost is not what you pay — it's what you give up. This sharpens Trade-offs by forcing you to name the specific alternative, not just acknowledge one exists.
*Catches:* Making choices without naming what specifically you're giving up; undervaluing the foregone option; treating "free" choices as free when the alternative is valuable.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the best thing you're not doing by doing this? Have you named it specifically?"

**Comparative Advantage**
Do what you're relatively better at, even if someone else is absolutely better at it. Value comes from relative advantage, not absolute skill.
*Catches:* Doing things because you're good at them rather than because you're relatively best at them; building a team with overlapping strengths; not outsourcing things where your comparative advantage lies elsewhere.
*Sparring prompt:* "What is your comparative advantage here? Is this the highest-relative-value use of your effort?"

**Externalities**
Costs or benefits that don't appear in the transaction but affect others. Every decision creates effects beyond its intended target.
*Catches:* Missing costs borne by stakeholders not in the room; ignoring second- and third-party consequences; failing to see ecosystem value being created or destroyed.
*Sparring prompt:* "Who bears a cost here who isn't part of this decision? What happens when they respond?"

**Gresham's Law**
Bad drives out good when both coexist in a system without differentiation. Tolerated mediocrity degrades everything around it over time.
*Catches:* Assuming quality will self-assert against mediocrity; watching good people exit as poor performance is tolerated; letting bad incentives contaminate good ones.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is the bad version quietly crowding out the good version? What happens if you let that run?"

**Creative Destruction**
New paradigms replace old ones. Disruption is not an anomaly — it is the default state of competitive systems operating over time.
*Catches:* Optimizing the current paradigm while a new one forms elsewhere; investing in moats that are being circumvented; confusing category leadership with safety.
*Sparring prompt:* "What would make this entire approach obsolete? Is that thing already starting somewhere?"

**Scarcity**
Resources are finite. Decisions are always allocations. What you do with scarce time, money, and attention is always a choice about what you don't do.
*Catches:* Planning without accounting for what's being deprioritized; treating "we'll figure that out" as free; failing to see that choosing everything is choosing nothing.
*Sparring prompt:* "What is genuinely scarce in this situation, and how are you making that trade-off explicit?"

**Supply and Demand**
Value is not intrinsic — it's relative to what's available and what's wanted. Price and availability are governed by the tension between supply and desire.
*Catches:* Building supply without validating demand; pricing without understanding elasticity; over-investing in a feature no one is pulling for.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the actual demand signal here? Are you building supply for a demand that exists, or one you're assuming?"

**Game Theory**
Your outcome depends on others' choices, and they know yours. Strategic interaction where moves and counter-moves are interdependent — not independent.
*Catches:* Optimizing in isolation; ignoring how competitors or partners will respond; treating cooperative and competitive situations with the same logic.
*Sparring prompt:* "What does the other player do when you make this move? Have you planned for their response?"

**Network Effects**
Value of a network grows disproportionately as more nodes join. Each new participant makes it more valuable for all existing ones.
*Catches:* Building platforms without a network effect strategy; assuming your product has network effects when it's really just scaling; not knowing if value grows with users or merely alongside them.
*Sparring prompt:* "Does this product get more valuable as more people use it — or does it just get bigger? What's the mechanism?"

**Narrative**
Humans construct causal stories from sequential events — often before causality is established. The story feels true because it's coherent, not because it's correct. This is how strategy decks, investor pitches, and post-mortems mislead.
*Catches:* Mistaking a coherent story for a proven one; building analysis on narrative rather than mechanism; being sold a pitch that sounds good without asking for the evidence underneath it.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is this a story or a proof? What evidence would exist if this narrative is actually correct?"
