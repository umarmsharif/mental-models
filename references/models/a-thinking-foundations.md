# Category A — Thinking Foundations

*How to reason, perceive, and form beliefs accurately.*

Owned by the **Thinking Foundations panel agent** in panel mode. 13 models. Each entry: what it is, what blindspot it catches, the sparring prompt that weaponises it. In solo mode, loaded directly as part of the deep bench.

---

**Map is not the Territory**
Your model of reality is not reality. Every strategy doc, framework, and plan is an abstraction that leaves things out. The more confident you are in your map, the more dangerous the gaps become.
*Catches:* Mistaking your strategy deck for what will happen on the ground; building on assumptions that feel like facts; treating last year's model as the current operating reality.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's in reality that your map of this situation doesn't include?"

**Circle of Competence**
You have a zone where your judgment is reliable and a zone where it isn't. Knowing the edge of your circle matters more than knowing what's inside it.
*Catches:* Expanding decisions into areas where you lack real pattern recognition; overconfidence from adjacent success; not knowing what you don't know.
*Sparring prompt:* "Are you making this call from inside your circle, or just near it?"

**First Principles Thinking**
Break an idea down to its fundamental truths, then rebuild from there. Don't inherit assumptions — decompose until you hit bedrock, then reason back up.
*Catches:* Working within inherited constraints that aren't real; copying playbooks that don't fit your context; accepting "that's how it's done" as a reason.
*Sparring prompt:* "If you had to build this from zero, knowing what you know now, what would you actually do?"

**Inversion**
Instead of asking "how do I succeed?" ask "how do I guarantee failure?" Then don't do that. Most failure modes are visible from the other end.
*Catches:* Only thinking forward; missing obvious failure modes that become clear when approached from the end; optimism bias in planning.
*Sparring prompt:* "What would make this definitely fail? Are you doing any of those things right now?"

**Second-Order Thinking**
First-order: what happens. Second-order: what happens as a result of what happens. Most people stop at first-order.
*Catches:* Ignoring indirect effects, rebound effects, and cascading consequences; being surprised by outcomes predictable one step out.
*Sparring prompt:* "If this works exactly as planned, what do you now have to deal with that you didn't before?"

**Probabilistic Thinking / Uncertainty**
Beliefs are probability distributions, not binary. Most confident people are compressed distributions pretending to be certainties.
*Catches:* Treating beliefs as facts; planning without scenario weighting; overconfidence in point estimates; false precision.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's your confidence interval on this, and what would shift it?"

**Bayesian Updating**
Revise beliefs in proportion to how much new evidence actually changes the probability — not by how surprising the evidence feels. The question is never "did I learn something new?" but "how much should this move my view?"
*Catches:* Overreacting to a single data point; anchoring to a prior and ignoring disconfirming evidence; treating all new information as equally significant; updating too little or too much.
*Sparring prompt:* "How much should this new information actually shift your view? What's your prior, and by how much does this move it?"

**Occam's Razor**
When two explanations fit the data equally well, prefer the simpler one. Complexity added without explanatory gain is noise.
*Catches:* Over-engineering explanations; adding layers to mask uncertainty; confusing sophistication with accuracy; complexity as a way of avoiding commitment.
*Sparring prompt:* "Is there a simpler explanation that fits the same facts?"

**Hanlon's Razor**
Never attribute to malice what can be adequately explained by incompetence, ignorance, or miscommunication.
*Catches:* Burning relationships by assuming bad intent; fighting an enemy that doesn't exist; wasting energy on conspiracy when confusion is the actual cause.
*Sparring prompt:* "Are you assuming bad intent here? What if they're just wrong or unaware?"

**Causation vs. Correlation**
Two things moving together doesn't mean one causes the other. The mechanism matters. Without a causal story, correlation is coincidence with good PR.
*Catches:* Acting on pattern-matching without a causal mechanism; building strategy on spurious correlations; misattributing growth or decline to the wrong driver.
*Sparring prompt:* "What's the mechanism? How does A actually produce B?"

**Framing**
The way information is presented shapes how it's perceived and what decisions follow. The same fact, framed differently, produces different choices. This applies to how you present to clients, how you structure a deck, and how you frame a problem internally.
*Catches:* Presenting data without noticing how the frame shapes the conclusion; being manipulated by how a problem is framed to you; discovering your own analysis is framed to confirm a prior you haven't examined.
*Sparring prompt:* "How is this framed? What would the same information look like from a different frame?"

**Devil's Advocate**
Deliberately steelman the opposing position to find what you haven't considered. The goal isn't balance — it's finding the argument that could undo you.
*Catches:* One-sided thinking; assembling only confirming evidence; missing the strongest version of the counter-argument.
*Sparring prompt:* "Make the best possible case against your own thesis."

**Falsifiability**
A belief that can't be proven wrong isn't a belief — it's a bias with a story. Ask: what would change your mind?
*Catches:* Unfalsifiable strategies; confirmation loops where no evidence could dislodge the thesis; mistaking resilience for correctness.
*Sparring prompt:* "What evidence, if you saw it, would make you abandon this thesis?"
